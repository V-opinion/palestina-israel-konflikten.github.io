Conflict Management and Conflict Resolution Model

Conflict management requires addressing complex intersections of religion, state, and democratic participation. The Multi-Religion Election System proposes a framework to address these issues. Key areas of focus include:

  • Conflict management regarding the religious who do not participate in the parliamentary elections or the EU elections.
  • Conflict management regarding the non-Protestant religious communities in the Nordic countries who oppose being equal with the Protestant religious communities despite being citizens of these countries. How should we handle this contradiction?
  • Conflict management regarding the Protestant civil servants who strongly criticize Islam and who oppose reforming the church elections in which they have the right to vote.
  • Conflict management regarding the Protestant faith communities that convert refugees to Protestantism.
  • Conflict management regarding the majority of EU states that lack corresponding church elections that have introduced democracy in their faith communities.
  • Conflict management regarding the Christian religious parties that represent Christians religiously both in the EU Parliament and in the Riksdag.

Conflict Management Regarding the Catholic Church

Hierarchical Structure (Apostolic Succession)

The Catholic Church is strictly hierarchical. Power does not emanate from the members (bottom-up) but from the Pope and the bishops (top-down).

Attitude Towards the Reform

Strongly rejecting. Introducing general elections where members vote on doctrines or leaders would violate the fundamental canon law of the church. The church believes that spiritual truths and leadership are not subject to majority votes or party-political election campaigns. Conflict Management Regarding Orthodox and Eastern Churches

Synodal and Episcopal Structure

Orthodox churches (e.g., Syriac Orthodox or Greek Orthodox) are governed by holy synods and bishops. Although lay people have some influence over local finances, the spiritual and administrative leadership is firmly anchored in the clerical hierarchy.

Attitude to the Reform

Strongly rejecting. The proposal would be seen as a serious infringement on the churches' autonomy. Since many of these communities have roots in countries where they have been persecuted by the state, they are extremely wary of secular states trying to reshape their internal structures.

Conflict Management Regarding Islamic Religious Communities

Theological Diversity and Independent Foundations

Islam lacks a centralized church structure or clergy like Christianity. Mosques are often organized as independent associations or foundations under various umbrella organizations. The leadership (including imams) is appointed based on theological competence, not through referendums. Listen to our Spotify podcast on conflict management for deeper insights.

Attitude to the Reform

Completely rejecting. To be forced into a structure that mimics a Protestant synod would be seen as a forced "Christification" or assimilation of Islamic traditions. It would also clash with how Islamic law (Sharia) and tradition view religious authority.

Summary Analysis

For non-Protestant religious communities, the proposal falls on the grounds that it is based on a Protestant and secular logic where religion is equated with democratic political parties.

  • Freedom of religion: The non-Protestant communities would point to the European Convention. The state may not interfere in the internal organization or teachings of a religious community. Church elections in the Nordic countries and the participation of political parties, etc.
  • Economic and democratic conditions: To condition state subsidies given from tax funds to non-Protestant religious communities to be compared with Protestant religious communities that are independent of tax funds but have nevertheless introduced democracy in their religious communities.

Nordic Protestant Faith Communities' Response to Reform

To understand how the Nordic Protestant faith communities would respond to a reform of a multi-faith electoral system, one must divide the analysis into two parts: the democratic and gender equality part, introducing democracy into their faith communities through church elections, allowing young people who have reached the age of 16 to practice democracy two years before the general election, and making it possible for a woman to become an archbishop.
