Conflict Management and Conflict Resolution Model

The conflict management and conflict resolution model are based on two main arguments:

  1. The 78-year-long conflict between Israel and Palestine primarily affects the entire Middle East: people are dying, and there is extensive environmental destruction, air pollution, and water pollution that affects both plants and animals, etc.
  2. Since two peace laureates who participated in the peace process between Israel and Palestine were murdered by religious extremists, no one has dared to get involved in that conflict. It has been allowed to continue for 78 years and has been handed down from one generation to the next. With this, we can conclude that this is a religious conflict alongside occupation and settlements, which has developed into a genocide in Gaza.

These two reasons give rise to the church election reform and the introduction of a Multi-Religion Election System.

The Core Questions of the Model

The two arguments are based on the following questions:

  • a) Do Protestant young people who have reached the age of 16 and who have the right to vote in church elections want to be equal to other 16-year-olds belonging to other faiths such as Catholics and Orthodox Christians, Jews, Muslims, Buddhists, and Hindus?
  • b) Do the Protestant female archbishops in the Nordic countries want to help so that other women belonging to other faiths also become equal, giving them the opportunity to become female leaders in the same way as men?
  • c) Do all religions want to cooperate democratically so that the 78-year-old conflict that they have inherited is not handed over to the next generation to be resolved? (Peace)

Analyzing Faith Communities and Church Election Reform

Since the religious communities in the Nordic countries have not officially commented on this specific theoretical proposal—a transition from church elections to a multi-religious electoral system—their positions can be analyzed and handled based on how they historically and theologically approach external democratization and state control. Conflict Management Regarding the Catholic Church

Hierarchical Structure (Apostolic Succession)

The Catholic Church is strictly hierarchical. Power does not originate from the members (bottom-up) but from the Pope and bishops (top-down).

Attitude Towards the Reform

Strongly rejecting. Introducing democracy in their religious communities, similar to the church elections in the Nordic countries where members vote on doctrines or leaders, would violate the church's fundamental canon law. The church believes that spiritual truths and leadership are not subject to majority votes or party-political election campaigns.

Conflict Management Regarding Orthodox and Eastern Churches

Synodal and Episcopal Structure

Orthodox churches (e.g., Syriac Orthodox or Greek Orthodox) are governed by holy synods and bishops. Although lay people have some influence over local finances, spiritual and administrative leadership is firmly anchored in the priestly hierarchy.

Attitude Towards the Reform

Strongly rejecting. The proposal would be seen as a serious infringement on the churches' autonomy. Since many of these communities have roots in countries where they have been persecuted by the state, they are extremely vigilant against secular states trying to reshape their internal structures. Conflict Management Regarding the Jewish Faith Communities

Attitude to Reform

Skeptical or dismissive. Even though they have democratic structures, they would oppose a state-standardized "multi-faith election." Judaism emphasizes the right of minorities to self-determination, and to introduce democracy in their religious communities similar to church elections—a reform that dictates how elections are to be held and financed—would be seen as a threat to Judaism. Summary Analysis

For non-Protestant religious communities, the proposal falls on the fact that it is based on a Protestant and secular logic where religion is equated in terms of democracy and equality.

  • Freedom of religion: The non-Protestant communities should point to the European Convention. The state may not interfere in the internal organization or teachings of a religious community. Church elections in the Nordic countries are to be equated with Protestant religious communities.
  • Economic and democratic conditions: To impose conditions instead of giving state grants taken from tax funds to non-Protestant religious communities is to be compared with Protestant religious communities that are independent of tax funds but have nevertheless introduced democracy in their religious communities.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What are the two main arguments of the MRES conflict resolution model?

1) The 78-year-long conflict between Israel and Palestine causes extensive environmental destruction and loss of life. 2) Because peace laureates were murdered by religious extremists, the conflict has been allowed to continue for 78 years, proving it is a religious conflict alongside occupation and settlements.

How do non-Protestant communities view the church election reform?

Non-Protestant communities generally reject the reform, citing the European Convention on freedom of religion. They argue that the state may not interfere in the internal organization or teachings of a religious community.
